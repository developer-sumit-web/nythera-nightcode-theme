# Nythera Nightcode — Case Study

## Goal

Create a dark theme that stays comfortable during long coding sessions while keeping syntax clearly distinguishable.

---

## Problem

Most dark themes fall into one of two extremes:

- High contrast → looks sharp but causes eye strain over time
- Low contrast → comfortable but reduces syntax clarity

During long sessions, both approaches break down.

---

## Approach

Instead of increasing or decreasing contrast globally, the focus was on:

- **Dimmed palette**
  Reduce intensity of colors rather than making them darker or brighter

- **Controlled contrast**
  Maintain enough separation between tokens without creating harsh highlights

- **Consistent behavior**
  Ensure similar elements (functions, variables, keywords) feel predictable across languages

---

## Key Decisions

### 1. No pure white highlights

Bright whites were avoided to prevent visual fatigue during extended use.

### 2. Warm color system

Cold, saturated tones were reduced in favor of warmer, more stable colors.

### 3. Balance over vibrancy

Clarity was prioritized over visual impact.

The theme is not designed to “pop” — it is designed to remain usable.

---

## Tradeoffs

- Slightly reduced vibrancy compared to high-contrast themes
- Less visual “pop” in exchange for long-session comfort
- Some users may prefer brighter themes for short bursts of work

---

## Iteration

The theme was refined through multiple adjustments:

- Rebalancing color intensity across UI and syntax
- Improving distinction between similar tokens
- Simplifying UI appearance to reduce visual noise
- Aligning documentation and preview with actual usage

---

## Result

A theme that:

- Remains stable over long coding sessions
- Maintains clear syntax distinction without harsh contrast
- Feels consistent across different languages and file types

---

## What I Learned

- Visual comfort is not just about lowering brightness — it requires balance
- Small color adjustments can significantly affect readability
- Consistency matters more than individual color choices
- Presentation (README, previews) affects how the work is perceived
